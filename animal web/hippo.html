HIPPOPOTAMUS

A hippopotamus is a large, semi-aquatic mammal from Africa with a barrel-shaped body, short legs, and a large head with a huge mouth. They have thick, grayish-brown skin that is nearly hairless and spend most of their day in water or mud to stay cool. Hippos emerge at night to graze on grasses and are known for their large teeth and surprisingly fast speed on land. Physical characteristics Body: Large, heavy, and barrel-shaped with short, stout legs and four-toed feet. Skin: Thick (about 2 inches), grayish-brown on top, and paler on the underside, with very little hair. It secretes a reddish-orange substance that acts as a natural sunscreen. Head: Enormous, with eyes, ears, and nostrils located high on the head to allow them to see and breathe while submerged. Mouth: Can open extremely wide to display large, sharp canine teeth, which can be over 12 inches long. Habitat and behavior Habitat: Rivers, lakes, and mangrove swamps in sub-Saharan Africa. Daily life: Spend days submerged in water or mud to stay cool and protect their skin from the sun, emerging at night to graze on grasses. Social structure: Live in groups called pods, which can contain from a few to over 100 individuals. Communication: Make loud grunts and roars to communicate. Movement: Can hold their breath for up to five minutes and can walk underwater. They are also capable of running surprisingly fast on land, up to 19 mph. Diet and reproduction Diet: Herbivores that primarily eat short grasses. Reproduction: Mating and giving birth occur in the water. Young hippos, called calves, can swim and nurse underwater.
