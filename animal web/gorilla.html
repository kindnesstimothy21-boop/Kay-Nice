GORILLA

A gorilla is the largest of the great apes, a large, powerful, and primarily herbivorous primate with a stocky body, broad chest, and a coat of dark hair. Adult males, called "silverbacks," are larger than females and develop a patch of silver hair on their backs as they age. Gorillas live in family groups in the forests of equatorial Africa and are known for being intelligent, social, and peaceful animals. Physical characteristics: They have muscular bodies, with arms that are longer than their legs, and large, human-like hands. Males can weigh up to 300-600 pounds, while females are about half that size. Adult males have a bony crest on their head and the signature silver patch on their lower back, earning them the name "silverback". They are covered in a coat of dark hair, except on their faces, hands, and feet. Behavior and diet: Gorillas are predominantly herbivores, primarily eating leaves, stems, and fruits, though they may occasionally eat insects. They are social animals that live in family groups called troops led by a silverback. They communicate through a variety of vocalizations and use their strength for foraging, nest building, and protecting their troop. They walk on their knuckles, a method called knuckle-walking. Gorillas are intelligent and emotional creatures, capable of forming strong family bonds. Habitat and conservation: Gorillas are found in the tropical and subtropical forests of central Africa. Sadly, all species of gorillas are endangered due to threats such as habitat loss and poaching.
