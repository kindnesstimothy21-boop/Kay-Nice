GIRAFFE

A giraffe is the world's tallest land animal, known for its long neck and legs, and a coat with a unique pattern of brown spots. These African herbivores use their long necks to reach leaves high in trees. They have hair-covered horns on their heads called ossicones and are social animals that live in groups called towers. Physical appearance: Giraffes are characterized by their extreme height, long necks (up to 6 feet), and long legs. Their coat is a buff or white background with a unique pattern of brown blotches or spots. They also have hair-covered horns on their heads called ossicones. Diet: As herbivores, giraffes eat plants, particularly leaves and twigs from acacia and other trees. Their long necks and long tongues (up to 18 inches) allow them to reach their favorite foods high in the canopy. Habitat: Giraffes live in various habitats across Africa, including savannas and woodlands. Social behavior: They are highly social animals that live in loose, dynamic groups known as "towers". Other facts: Despite their long necks, giraffes have the same number of cervical vertebrae as humans (seven), but each one is much larger (up to 25 cm). They can run at speeds of up to 35 miles per hour over short distances. They have very short sleep requirements, needing only 10 minutes to 2 hours of sleep per day. They have a gentle and friendly nature.
