ZEBRA

A zebra is a horse-like mammal from Africa, distinguished by its black and white stripes, with each individual having a unique pattern. These animals are herbivores that live in herds, possess excellent eyesight and hearing, and are known for their ability to run at high speeds to escape predators like lions. Physical characteristics Body: Similar in build to a horse, with a barrel-chested body, long legs, and a long neck. Mane: They have an upright, erect mane that runs along the neck. Stripes: Covered in a distinctive pattern of black and white stripes, which are unique to each zebra, like fingerprints. The stripes are thought to provide camouflage and deter insects. Tail: A tufted tail. Height: Typically stand between 47 and 55 inches (120 and 140 cm) tall at the shoulder. Habitat and diet Habitat: Native to Africa, zebras live in various habitats, including grasslands and savannas. Diet: They are herbivores, primarily eating grass, but they also consume shrubs, leaves, and bark. Behavior and social structure Social life: Zebras are social animals that live in herds, which often form family units consisting of one male, several females, and their young. Social structure: This social structure is more stable in some species (like the plains and mountain zebras) compared to others (like the Grevy's zebra). Defense: They can be aggressive and will use strong kicks to defend themselves from predators. Other notable facts Species: There are three species of zebra: Grevy's zebra, mountain zebra, and plains zebra. Relation: They are closely related to horses and donkeys. Speed: Capable of running up to 35 miles per hour (56 km/h).
