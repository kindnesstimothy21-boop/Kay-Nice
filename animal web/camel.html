CAMEL

A camel is a large, hoofed mammal famous for its ability to survive long periods without water, known for its humps which store fat, long neck, and adaptations for desert life. There are two main types: the one-humped dromedary and the two-humped Bactrian camel. Camels have thick fur for insulation, long eyelashes and closable nostrils to protect against sand, and wide, padded feet for walking on sand. Physical characteristics Humps: Camels have either one (dromedary) or two (Bactrian) humps that store fat for energy, not water. Size: They are large mammals, typically standing 5.2 to 6.6 feet tall at the shoulder and weighing 800 to 1,300 pounds. Fur: Their coats vary in color from gray to brown and can be long and shaggy or shorter depending on the species and season. Feet: They have two-toed feet with broad, padded soles that help them walk on sand without sinking. Mouth: A split upper lip and toughened mouth lining allow them to eat thorny, prickly plants. Other features: They have long, curved necks, long eyelashes, and the ability to close their nostrils to keep out sand. Adaptations for survival Water conservation: Camels can go for long periods without drinking and can consume large amounts of water at once to stay hydrated for days. Temperature regulation: Their thick fur insulates them from both the heat of the sun and the cold desert nights, and they can regulate their body temperature. Sand protection: Long eyelashes, ear hairs, and closable nostrils provide protection from sandstorms. Efficient digestion: Their three-chambered stomachs are highly efficient at breaking down tough, dry plant matter. Other facts Domestication: Camels have been domesticated for thousands of years and are used for transportation, milk, and meat. Lifespan: They can live for 40 to 50 years. Diet: They are herbivores that primarily eat shrubs, grasses, and other desert vegetation.
