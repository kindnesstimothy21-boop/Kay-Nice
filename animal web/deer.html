DEER

A deer is a four-legged, hoofed ruminant that typically has a compact body, long ears, a short tail, and a coat of fur that changes color with the seasons. Males of most species, called bucks, have antlers that are shed and regrown annually, while the females are called does. They are herbivores that live in diverse habitats and are known for their ability to leap, jump, and their heightened senses, particularly at dawn and dusk. Physical characteristics Body: A compact body with a short tail and long, slender legs. Hooves: Each foot has two large and two small hooves. Coat: The color varies by season and species. In summer, it's often reddish-brown, while in winter it becomes a duller grayish-brown. Some species have spots, especially as fawns. Antlers: Found on males of most species and females of some (like reindeer). They are bony growths that are shed and regrown each year, unlike permanent horns. Size: Deer range from the tiny pudu to the massive moose. Habitat and diet Habitat: Found in diverse environments worldwide, including forests, tundra, mountains, and swamps, and often adapt to human-modified landscapes. Diet: Herbivores that eat a variety of vegetation, such as grasses, leaves, twigs, buds, and fruits. Behavior and senses Activity: Often most active at dawn and dusk. Movement: Capable of impressive jumps, both vertically and horizontally. Senses: Possess excellent hearing and a strong sense of smell, which helps them detect predators. Social structure: Many live in herds, which can vary in size. Classification Family: Cervidae. Order: Artiodactyla (even-toed ungulates).
