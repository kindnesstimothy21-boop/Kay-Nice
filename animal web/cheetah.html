CHEETAH

A cheetah is a slender, long-legged cat with a distinctive tawny coat covered in solid black spots. Known as the fastest land animal, it has a lightweight build, a flexible spine, and powerful hind legs, all adapted for speed. Key identifying features include black tear-like marks running from the eyes to the mouth and blunt, semi-retractable claws. Physical characteristics Build: Light and slender, with a small head, deep chest, long legs, and a long tail. Coat: Sandy yellow or tan with small, solid black spots covering the body. The underside is lighter. Each cheetah has a unique spot pattern. Head: Small and rounded, with short ears. Black "tear-like" lines run from the inner corner of each eye down to the mouth, helping to reduce sun glare. Claws: Blunt and semi-retractable, unlike the fully retractable claws of most cats, which provide extra grip while running. Tail: Long and spotted, with four to six dark rings at the end, followed by a bushy white tip. The tail acts as a rudder for balance during high-speed turns. Vocalizations: Cheetahs cannot roar; they purr and make other sounds like chirps, hisses, and growls. Habitat and behavior Habitat: Primarily found in the savannas and grasslands of Africa and parts of Iran. Speed: Capable of reaching speeds up to 70 mph (112 kph) for short bursts, making it the fastest land animal. Hunting: They stalk prey and use their speed for short, high-speed chases. As carnivores, they hunt animals like gazelles and impalas. Social structure: Females are typically solitary, while males may form small, lifelong coalitions with their brothers. Activity: They are mainly diurnal (active during the day), especially in the early mornings or late afternoons.
