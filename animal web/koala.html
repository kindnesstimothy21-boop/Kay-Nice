KOALA

A koala is a tree-dwelling, herbivorous marsupial native to Australia, known for its stout, grey, furry body, large ears, and big black nose. They are excellent climbers due to their sharp claws and unique paws, which have opposable digits for gripping branches. As a marsupial, koalas carry their young in a pouch, and their diet consists almost exclusively of eucalyptus leaves. Physical characteristics Body: A stout, tailless body covered in thick, soft fur that ranges from light gray to brown. Face: A round head with large, fluffy ears and a broad, black, leathery nose. Hands and Feet: Sharp claws and specialized paws with multiple opposable digits, allowing them to grip branches effectively. Size: Adults typically range from 24 to 33 inches (60–85 cm) long and can weigh between 4 and 15 kg (8.8–33.1 lbs), with larger koalas found in southern Australia. Unique features: They have fingerprints similar to humans and a hard, padded rump to help them sit comfortably in tree forks. Diet and habitat Habitat: Primarily lives in eucalyptus trees in the coastal forests of eastern and southern Australia. Diet: Feeds almost exclusively on eucalyptus leaves, which it chews with strong jaws and teeth. Its digestive system is adapted to process these leaves, which contain toxins. Water: Gets most of its water from the eucalyptus leaves it eats, but will drink water when necessary. Lifestyle and behavior Activity: Is primarily nocturnal and sleeps for long periods, sometimes up to 19 hours a day, to conserve energy. Reproduction: Is a marsupial, meaning it gives birth to a very undeveloped young called a joey, which is then carried in a pouch to finish developing. Social behavior: Males have scent glands on their chests to mark trees and attract females.
